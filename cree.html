Cree

Photo of Chief Starblanket and group of men at Treaty Four Signing.

The Cree are a prominent group in western Canada, the most populous and widely distributed group in the entirety of Canada. They believed that animals were people and had intelligence and spirit, and whenever they hunted, they viewed their relationship with animals as one of reciprocity.

Shamans in Cree Culture were seen with respect, for they were in charge of a variety of customs related to the spirits of game animals throughout Cree culture. The Cree are split into two major divisions, both being American subarctic peoples.

Christian Influence and Cultural Disruption

In the early 20th century, the majority of Cree became Christians. Some practices, such as polygamy and the “shaking tent” conjuring ceremony, were abandoned. The missionaries also banned hunting on Sundays, changed burial customs, and prevented them from doing proper hunting rites.

The Cree children were taken to residential schools, forcibly being removed, and were taught farming and housework instead of traditional Cree religion.

Resistance and Cultural Revival

In spite of colonization, Cree people have preserved many aspects of their spirituality through quiet resistance and cultural resilience. The primary method of resistance against Christianity was instead to adopt a hybrid worldview. One example is Maskepetoon, a Cree leader in the Fort Edmonton area who formed alliances with Methodist missionaries like McDouggal and Woolsey. He did this intending to allow his band to retain access to the buffalo, using the missionaries for survival purposes instead of converting.

Other Cree maintained their spirituality through hiding their traditional ceremonies, or through the synthesis of their culture through similarities.







Photo of Maskepetoon, a Cree leader.

Significance

The Cree’s blending of Christian and traditional beliefs helps demonstrate how Indigenous communities were forced to adapt to colonialism, balancing their survival with cultural preservation. It helps show the Cree’s strength, shaping their own future despite outside pressures. Recognizing this helps us understand the importance of Cultural revival efforts and the need to support Indigenous rights.
